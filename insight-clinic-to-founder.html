The Unexpected Path to Entrepreneurship

When I started my career in healthcare, I never imagined I would become an entrepreneur founding an e-commerce startup. My journey was rooted in clinical practice‚Äîworking across multiple clinics in Kathmandu, managing operations, and directly serving patients. Yet this experience became the foundation for recognizing opportunities and building a business mindset.

The transition from clinical practice to entrepreneurship wasn't a sudden leap; it was a gradual awakening to the systemic problems in healthcare delivery that could only be solved through business innovation. Every challenge I faced in clinics‚Äî supply chain issues, cost management, operational efficiency‚Äîwas actually a business problem waiting for an entrepreneurial solution.

What Clinic Management Taught Me About Business

1. Operations Matter More Than You Think

Running multiple clinics taught me that excellence isn't just about clinical skills‚Äîit's about logistics, inventory management, staff coordination, and operational efficiency. These are the unglamorous but critical aspects of any business. A perfectly skilled healthcare provider in a poorly managed clinic delivers mediocre results. Conversely, excellent operations amplify clinical quality.

2. Customer Experience Is King

In clinics, every patient interaction matters. From the moment a patient enters to how their follow-up is managed, every touchpoint shapes their perception. This customer-centric mindset is equally critical in startups. Whether you're serving patients or clinic partners, understanding their needs and exceeding expectations is what builds loyalty and sustainable growth.

3. Cost Efficiency Without Compromising Quality

Clinics operate on tight margins. Managing costs‚Äîfrom supplies to staff‚Äîwithout sacrificing quality taught me the fine art of resource optimization. This principle directly translates to startup management. How do you scale without burning cash? How do you deliver premium quality at competitive prices? These were clinic problems that became startup problems.

4. Relationships Are Your Most Valuable Asset

Success in clinic management relied heavily on relationships‚Äîwith suppliers, staff, patients, and peers. As an entrepreneur, I quickly realized that these same relationship-building skills are crucial. Your network is your net worth. Every clinic partner, every industry contact, and every patient testimonial became part of the ecosystem that supports a startup.

The Entrepreneurial Mindset

"Entrepreneurship isn't just about having a good idea. It's about the relentless pursuit of solving a problem better than anyone else, at scale, and with sustainable economics."

An entrepreneurial mindset consists of several key characteristics:

  • Problem-First Thinking: Identify real problems before creating solutions. Mediizo emerged from observing actual gaps in healthcare supply chains, not from a desire to build an e-commerce platform.
  • Resilience: Startups face constant obstacles. Clinic management prepared me to handle crises, adapt quickly, and find solutions under pressure.
  • Continuous Learning: The healthcare environment changes constantly. This taught me the importance of staying informed, adapting to change, and never assuming you have all the answers.
  • Risk Assessment: Every clinical decision involves weighing risks and benefits. In business, this becomes strategic risk management‚Äîunderstanding what risks are worth taking and which ones could be fatal.
  • Customer Obsession: Ultimately, success is determined by whether customers find value in what you offer. This obsessive focus on value delivery drives every decision in both clinical and entrepreneurial contexts.

From Managing Clinics to Managing a Startup

While clinic management and startup founding share many principles, they also differ significantly. As a clinic manager, I had established revenue streams, known operational procedures, and relatively predictable outcomes. As a founder, I'm operating in uncertainty, managing limited resources, and constantly pivoting based on market feedback.

However, the fundamentals remain constant: solve real problems, operate efficiently, treat people well, and continuously improve. These principles work whether you're managing a 10-person clinic or scaling a startup to serve thousands.

Lessons for Aspiring Healthcare Entrepreneurs

Get Your Hands Dirty First

Before starting a healthcare business, work in the industry. Understand the challenges from the ground level. This practical experience is invaluable and something no MBA or business course can fully teach.

Listen More Than You Talk

When I started building Mediizo, I spent months talking to clinic owners, healthcare providers, and patients. Their feedback shaped the platform far more than my initial assumptions. Entrepreneurs who assume they know better than their customers often fail.

Think Long-Term While Acting Short-Term

Startups need to survive the next 3 months while building toward a 5-year vision. This balancing act is what separates successful founders from those who burn out or give up too early.

Build for Your Market, Not Your Ego

It's easy to build what you think is impressive. It's hard to build what your market actually needs and is willing to pay for. Mediizo's design came from market needs, not technological sophistication.

Looking Forward

As I continue to build Mediizo, I carry the lessons from every clinic I managed, every patient I served, and every challenge I overcame. These experiences aren't background‚Äîthey're the foundation upon which sustainable entrepreneurship is built.
